Vector projections and best approximation

For subspace WWW of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn with orthogonal basis {u⃗1,u⃗2,…,u⃗p}\{ \vec{u}_1, \vec{u}_2, \dots, \vec{u}_p \}{u1​,u2​,…,up​}, any vector y⃗\vec{y}y​ can be projected onto WWW via the following formula. y^⃗=projW(y⃗)=y⃗⋅u⃗1u⃗1⋅u⃗1u⃗1+y⃗⋅u⃗2u⃗2⋅u⃗2u⃗2+⋯+y⃗⋅u⃗pu⃗p⋅u⃗pu⃗p \vec{\hat{y}} = proj_W (\vec{y}) = \frac{\vec{y}\cdot\vec{u}_1}{\vec{u}_1 \cdot \vec{u}_1} \vec{u}_1 + \frac{\vec{y}\cdot\vec{u}_2}{\vec{u}_2 \cdot \vec{u}_2} \vec{u}_2 + \dots + \frac{\vec{y}\cdot\vec{u}_p}{\vec{u}_p \cdot \vec{u}_p} \vec{u}_p y^​​=projW​(y​)=u1​⋅u1​y​⋅u1​​u1​+u2​⋅u2​y​⋅u2​​u2​+⋯+up​⋅up​y​⋅up​​up​ This is illustrated in the below image.

It can be shown that projection y^⃗\vec{\hat{y}}y^​​ is the best approximation of y⃗\vec{y}y​ in subspace WWW. That is, ∥y⃗−y^⃗∥<∥y⃗−v⃗∥ \| \vec{y} - \vec{\hat{y}} \| < \| \vec{y} - \vec{v} \| ∥y​−y^​​∥<∥y​−v∥ for all v⃗\vec{v}v in WWW not equatl to y^⃗\vec{\hat{y}}y^​​. See section 5.3 of the text for details.

Least squares and the normal equations

For linear system Ax⃗=b⃗A \vec{x} = \vec{b}Ax=b with matrix AAA of dimension m×nm \times nm×n, if AAA has many more rows than columns (m>>n m >> nm>>n), there likely is no solution to this system of equations. That is, vector b⃗\vec{b}b is not in Col(A)Col(A)Col(A).

Instead of solving exactly, the best option is now finding a best approximation known as the least-squares solution. That is, we seek x^⃗∈Rn\vec{\hat{x}} \in \mathbb{R}^nx^∈Rn such that ∥b⃗−Ax^⃗∥≤∥b⃗−Ax⃗∥ \| \vec{b} - A \vec{\hat{x}} \| \leq \| \vec{b} - A \vec{x} \| ∥b−Ax^∥≤∥b−Ax∥ for all x⃗\vec{x}x in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn. This equates to solving the system Ax⃗=b^⃗A \vec{x} = \vec{\hat{b}}Ax=b^ where b^⃗\vec{\hat{b}}b^ is the projection of b⃗\vec{b}b onto the column space of AAA. That is, b^⃗=projCol(A)(b⃗) \vec{\hat{b}} = proj_{Col(A)} (\vec{b}) b^=projCol(A)​(b) as illustrated below.

Using the fundamental theorem of linear algebra, it can be shown that solving this least squares problem is equivalent to solving the normal equations ATAx⃗=ATb⃗. A^T A \vec{x} = A^T \vec{b}. ATAx=ATb. See section 6.5 of the text for details.

Linear regression and fitting lines to data

When analyzing data, it is useful to find relationships between variables. In class, we discussed house sales data and examined the relationship between price of a house and size (square feet) of the house. By assuming the relationship of the data is linear, one can quantify this relationship as the slope of a line. To find such a line, a least squares problem needs to be solved.

To relate two variables xxx and yyy as linear equation y=β0+β1x, y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x, y=β0​+β1​x, we need to find slope β1\beta_1β1​ and yyy-intercept β0\beta_0β0​ which best fits data (x0,y0),…,(xn,yn)(x_0, y_0), \dots, (x_n, y_n)(x0​,y0​),…,(xn​,yn​). Graphically, this is illustrated below.
